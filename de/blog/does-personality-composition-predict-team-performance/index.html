Does personality composition predict team performance? What 60 studies say

Big Five team performance across 60 studies confirms personality composition predicts outcomes — effect sizes are modest, task type is the key moderator.

Miquel Matoses·8 min read

Dieser Artikel ist nur auf Englisch verfügbar.

When organisations talk about "team fit," they usually mean culture and values. But there is a more specific, empirically tractable question underneath: does the distribution of personality traits across a team actually predict how well that team performs? The answer, based on more than six decades of accumulated research, is yes — but with important qualifications about effect sizes, task types, and what the evidence can and cannot tell us.

What Team Personality Composition Actually Measures

Individual personality predicts individual behaviour. Team personality composition refers to the aggregate properties of a team's collective personality profile — for example, the mean level of Conscientiousness (Discipline in Cèrcol) across all members, or the minimum level of Agreeableness (Bond) among any member. These aggregate indices are conceptually distinct from any individual's profile: a team can have a high mean Conscientiousness even if one member is low on the dimension, and that single low scorer may have different implications depending on which aggregation model you apply.

Researchers have explored several composition models. The mean-score model asks what the average level of a trait across the team predicts. The minimum-score model (sometimes called the "weakest-link" model) asks whether the team's lowest scorer on a critical trait acts as a bottleneck — the logic being that in interdependent tasks, the team can only move as fast as its least organised or least cooperative member. The variance model asks whether diversity of trait levels within the team moderates performance. Each model captures a different structural aspect of composition, and each makes different empirical predictions.

What Bell's 2007 Meta-Analysis of 60 Studies Found

The most comprehensive quantitative synthesis of this literature is Bell's (2007) meta-analysis, which integrated findings from 60 independent studies examining the relationship between "deep-level" team composition variables — including Big Five personality dimensions — and team performance.

Bell (2007), Journal of Applied Psychology: "Mean levels of Agreeableness and Conscientiousness, and minimum levels of Conscientiousness, were consistently related to team performance across studies." Mean Conscientiousness showed a corrected correlation with team performance of r ≈ .19; mean Agreeableness was similarly consistent. These effects were small to moderate by conventional standards but were replicated across diverse task types and samples. doi:10.1037/0021-9010.92.3.595

The 60 studies spanned laboratory teams, student project groups, and organisational work teams, covering a range of industries and task types. This breadth both strengthens the generalisability of the findings and introduces the task-type moderation question discussed below.

Which Big Five Dimensions Predict Team Performance?

Agreeableness (Bond)Strong and consistentHigher mean → better performance
Extraversion (Presence)Moderate, context-dependentHigher mean → better in social/dynamic roles
Openness to Experience (Vision)Moderate, task-dependentHigher mean → better in creative/complex tasks
Neuroticism (Depth)Moderate, negativeHigher mean → worse under pressure

Discipline (Conscientiousness) emerges as the most reliable predictor. The corrected mean-level correlation in Bell (2007) was r ≈ .19, and the minimum-level model showed similarly consistent effects — suggesting that having even one very low-Discipline member may impair team performance in highly interdependent tasks. This echoes Barrick & Mount's (1991) individual-level finding that Conscientiousness is the only Big Five dimension predicting job performance across all occupational categories.

Bond (Agreeableness) at the team level predicts cohesion and cooperation. Bell (2007) found this effect to be robust. The mechanism is intuitive: teams where members are generally trusting, warm, and cooperative find it easier to coordinate, resolve minor conflicts, and maintain working relationships under pressure.

Presence (Extraversion) is more context-dependent. In teams with high social demands — sales, client-facing work, performance contexts — higher mean Presence predicts better outcomes. In teams doing concentrated technical work, the relationship is weaker or absent.

Vision (Openness) is most relevant in creative, complex, and knowledge-intensive tasks. In routine or compliance-focused environments, it shows less predictive power.

Depth (Neuroticism) shows a negative relationship: teams where members average higher on emotional reactivity tend to perform worse under pressure. This does not mean emotionally reactive individuals are less valuable — it reflects aggregate effects under team stress.

Why Task Type Changes How Personality Predicts Performance

One of the most important findings in the team composition literature is that the personality-performance relationship is substantially moderated by task type. Bell (2007) found stronger effects for tasks requiring high interdependence and coordination, and weaker effects for independent or additive tasks where members contribute largely in parallel.

For creative and innovation-oriented tasks, Vision (Openness) becomes a stronger predictor. For execution-heavy tasks requiring sustained follow-through, Discipline (Conscientiousness) dominates. For teams under high interpersonal stress or conflict, Bond (Agreeableness) and Depth (Neuroticism) carry more predictive weight. Teams doing intellectually autonomous work — software engineering, research, writing — often show weaker composition effects overall than teams doing tightly interdependent procedural work.

This moderation is both theoretically coherent and practically important: the same personality composition that predicts success in one task context may be neutral or mildly negative in another.

Mean-Score vs. Minimum-Score: Which Model Fits Your Team?

The minimum-score (weakest-link) model has received empirical support for specific dimensions. Bell (2007) found evidence that minimum Conscientiousness — not just mean Conscientiousness — was a reliable predictor of team performance. The logic is intuitive: in a task requiring every team member to meet a deadline, the one member who consistently misses deadlines can impair the whole team's output regardless of how organised everyone else is.

The minimum model does not apply equally to all dimensions. Minimum Agreeableness has less consistent support than minimum Conscientiousness. Minimum Vision appears largely irrelevant: creative tasks benefit from high-Vision members even if other members are more conventional in their thinking.

The practical implication is that for Discipline (Conscientiousness), teams should pay attention not only to the average but to the floor. One very low-Discipline member in a highly interdependent team may limit what the team can achieve more than the mean suggests.

Critical Gaps: What Personality Composition Research Can't Tell You

Several important limitations apply to this body of evidence.

Causality is not established. Most studies are cross-sectional — personality and performance are measured at the same time or after the fact. Whether personality composition caused the performance differences, or whether high-performing teams attract particular personality profiles, or whether selection processes create the observed correlations, cannot be determined from cross-sectional data alone.

Context is underspecified. The 60 studies in Bell (2007) vary enormously in task type, team size, industry, and culture. Effect sizes vary accordingly. A mean r of .19 for Conscientiousness is an average across this heterogeneous pool — the true effect in any specific context may be substantially higher or lower.

Selection effects. In real organisations, personality is rarely the only input into team formation. Skill, experience, network, and availability all influence who ends up on which team. Isolating personality effects from these confounds is methodologically challenging.

Dynamic change. Teams change over time. Personality profiles may shift (slowly), relationships develop, and the relevance of different traits may vary as task demands evolve. Most composition research treats teams as static.

How to Apply Personality Composition Research in Practice

The research justifies treating personality composition as one meaningful input into team formation and development — not as a deterministic formula. Several practical guidelines follow from the evidence:

  • Prioritise Discipline (Conscientiousness) as a floor concern. In highly interdependent teams, one very low-Discipline member may have disproportionate impact. This is the dimension where the minimum-score logic has the most empirical support.
  • Use mean Bond (Agreeableness) as a baseline for cohesion risk. Teams with very low mean Agreeableness are predictively at risk of persistent interpersonal friction. Teams with very high mean Agreeableness may face groupthink risks (see our article on that topic).
  • Calibrate Vision (Openness) to task type. In creative and complex work, check whether the team has sufficient Vision. In execution-heavy work, it matters less.
  • Do not use personality data to exclude. Effect sizes are meaningful but not large. Personality composition explains some variance in performance — not all of it, and not independently of skills, context, and leadership. See our guide to building balanced teams for a fuller discussion.

Understanding what 60 studies actually say — including the nuances about effect sizes, task moderation, and model type — is more useful than any oversimplified prescription. The science supports personality composition as a real predictor. It does not support treating it as destiny.
