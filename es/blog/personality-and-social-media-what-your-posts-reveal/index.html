Personality and social media: what your posts reveal — and what they don't

In 2013, a team of researchers at the University of Cambridge published a study that would change the public conversation about personality and digital data. Michal Kosinski, David Stillwell, and Thore Graepel analysed a dataset of 58,000 Facebook users and found that the simple act of "liking" content on the platform — music, brands, political pages, television shows — predicted users' Big Five personality scores with meaningful accuracy. Extraversion, Openness, and Neuroticism were the best-predicted traits; correlations with self-report personality scores ranged up to approximately .40 for Extraversion.

The paper, published in Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences (DOI: 10.1073/pnas.1218772110), was not the first study to link social media behaviour with personality, but its scale and simplicity made it landmark. You do not need to write anything. You do not need to disclose anything. The pattern of content you respond to is itself a personality signal.

This finding opened a window onto a significant scientific question — and a significant ethical problem. A general introduction to social media as a behavioural environment is available at Wikipedia: Social media.

What Big Five Research Shows About Personality Signals in Social Media

The Kosinski et al. result has been replicated and extended across multiple platforms and methodologies. The broad picture is consistent: Big Five traits leave detectable traces in how people use social media, though the signal strength varies considerably by trait and by platform.

Extraversion (Presence). The most reliable personality signal in social media data. High-Extraversion users post more frequently, have larger social networks, use more social content (photos with friends, event attendance, references to social activities), and engage more with others' content through likes and comments. High-Extraversion profiles also contain more positive emotional language and references to social events and relationships. Effect sizes for Extraversion predictions from social media are consistently among the strongest in the literature, typically r = .30 to .45.

Openness to Experience (Vision). The second most reliably predicted trait. High-Openness users engage with more diverse content, are more likely to share articles about cultural, intellectual, or political topics, and use more complex and varied vocabulary in their posts. They are more likely to be early adopters of new platforms and features. Text-based models predict Openness from linguistic features with correlations of approximately .30 to .40.

Neuroticism (Depth). Moderately predictable. High-Neuroticism users tend to post more frequently during evenings and late nights, use more negative emotional vocabulary, share more content related to personal difficulties, and show more variable posting frequency. The signal is real but noisier than for Extraversion, partly because Neuroticism manifests differently in different individuals — some withdraw from social media during difficult periods, others increase their posting.

Conscientiousness (Discipline). Harder to detect from social media behaviour. High-Conscientiousness users tend to have more complete and consistent profiles, post with greater regularity, and engage in more structured content (task-completion posts, productivity content, professional updates). But the signal is weaker than for Extraversion and Openness, partly because the core behaviours that define Conscientiousness — reliability, follow-through, organisation — do not express themselves distinctively in social media activity.

Agreeableness (Bond). The hardest Big Five trait to detect from social media. High-Agreeableness users are more cooperative and less confrontational online, but the behavioural signatures are subtle and difficult to distinguish from Extraversion's social warmth or Conscientiousness's reliability. Predictions of Agreeableness from social media data are consistently the weakest across studies.

The Cambridge Analytica Case: How Personality Research Became Manipulation

"In 2016 we commenced a large global project to support the campaign... We did this through targeting — understanding how different people receive and respond to messages — and by finding the most persuadable voters in the key states." — Christopher Wylie, Cambridge Analytica whistleblower testimony, 2018

The Kosinski et al. research had an unintended sequel. In 2013, a researcher named Aleksandr Kogan built a Facebook quiz app that collected personality data from users and — crucially — from their friends' networks without explicit consent. The data was passed to Cambridge Analytica, a political consultancy that claimed to use psychographic profiling derived from Big Five scores to target political advertising in the 2016 US presidential election and the UK Brexit referendum.

The Cambridge Analytica case is important for two reasons. First, it demonstrated that the gap between academic research on personality inference and commercial deployment of that inference for manipulative purposes was far smaller than most researchers had anticipated. Second, it prompted a fundamental rethinking of consent frameworks for behavioural data. The fact that you use a platform does not constitute informed consent for your behavioural data to be used to infer personality and target you with psychographically tailored persuasive messages.

The case is also routinely used to illustrate a capability that the research actually does not support at the level Cambridge Analytica claimed. The academic correlations between social media behaviour and Big Five traits — even at their strongest — are modest enough that individual-level targeting based on personality profiles is substantially noisier than was publicly claimed. The company's effectiveness in individual-level psychographic micro-targeting was very likely overstated; what was not overstated was the scale of the privacy violation.

What Social Media Posts Cannot Reliably Reveal About Personality

The Kosinski findings, and the broader literature they represent, are frequently misinterpreted in two directions: either dismissed as privacy-threatening surveillance that can read your mind from your tweets, or dismissed as too weak to matter. Both framings are wrong.

What social media posts reliably predict is your position on broad personality dimensions, estimated with meaningful but limited accuracy, when aggregate data from many posts and interactions is available. What they do not predict is:

Specific intentions and decisions. Personality traits are distal predictors of behaviour. They explain variance in long-run tendencies, not specific acts. High-Extraversion scores do not tell you whether someone will attend a particular event, accept a particular job, or vote in a particular way. The signal degrades rapidly as you move from "general tendency" to "specific decision in a specific context."

Context-sensitive behaviour. People behave differently in different roles and contexts. The personality signal in someone's professional LinkedIn activity may differ substantially from the signal in their personal Twitter account — not because the underlying trait has changed, but because the same trait expresses differently in different social contexts. Merging data across contexts to infer a single "true" profile ignores this important reality.

Individual accuracy. The correlations that make personality prediction statistically significant at group level — r = .35, r = .40 — translate into prediction accuracy at the individual level that is substantially weaker than these numbers suggest. For any individual person, the uncertainty around a personality inference from social media is large. Group-level statistical patterns should not be treated as individual diagnoses.

Why Cèrcol Uses Witness Ratings Instead of Social Media Signals

The social media personality research demonstrates that behavioural data carries personality information. It does not demonstrate that personality inference from behavioural data is accurate enough, fair enough, or ethically grounded enough for individual-level assessment purposes.

Cèrcol's model is built on explicit participation: self-report combined with peer assessment from Witnesses who know the individual in context. The individual always controls their own data and knows what is being assessed and by whom. This approach sacrifices the novelty of passive inference in exchange for meaningfully higher accuracy at the individual level and a consent framework that is honest about what is being measured.

The social media research is scientifically valuable. It tells us that personality traits are real, that they shape behaviour consistently across contexts, and that the Big Five dimensions map onto distinct patterns of online behaviour. These are not trivial findings. But the distance from "detectable in aggregate social media data" to "appropriate for individual assessment" is larger than popular accounts typically acknowledge.
